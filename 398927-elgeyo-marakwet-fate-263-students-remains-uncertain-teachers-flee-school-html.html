Elgeyo Marakwet: Fate of 263 students remains uncertain as teachers flee school

- When parents took their children to Flourspar Primary School on Monday morning, they hoped to leave them settled in class

- A surprise, however, awaited them as they stayed on for hours but not a single teacher showed up; they were forced to take their pupils back home.

- Among the affected pupils were 17 candidates who did not know the way forward as their final primary school exam date neared

- The teachers were protesting a delay in salaries from January 2020 and vowed to stay away until their dues are sorted

As schools continue to reopen in Kenya after several months of closure due to COVID-19, the fate of 263 pupils at Fluorspar Day and Boarding Primary School, among them 17 candidates, hangs in the balance.

All the 14 teachers declined to report back to school on grounds that their salaries dating back to January 2020 were yet to be paid.

Fluorspar Primary School, located in Kerio Valley, Elgeyo-Marakwet county, is run by the Ministry of Petroleum and Mining.

The school’s operations were placed under the government agency after the initial owner, Kenya Fluorspar Company, closed shop in 2016 after running it since 1974.

At the time of their exit, the company had resourced the institution to exemplary standards, making it one of the most technologically advanced primary schools in Kenya.

According to the Ministry of Education, the primary school is still registered as a private institution therefore not within the government’s jurisdiction.

Talking to journalists, County Director of Education Kituyi Masibo explained that the conversion from private to public is ongoing, and the confusion is just but a teething problem that stakeholders should expect.

Teachers at the school felt left out as their colleagues in other schools continued to receive their monthly pay despite schools being closed.

The back and forth between the school’s management and government throws off-balance the future of 17 candidates set to sit their KCPE this year.

This is a second blow to the pupils who had to endure a 9-month long delay in taking their final primary school exams as learning institutions were closed indefinitely in March 2020 to mitigate against the spread of COVID-19.

When reached for comment, the chairman of the school’s Parents Association Hosea Toroitich indicated that the learners were forced to go back home.

He also added that the aggrieved teachers had resolved to remain put until their pending salaries are paid.
