Opinion: Kenyas clergy must reclaim pulpit power, walk in the footsteps of Gitari, Ndingi

The silence and passiveness of the church regarding the disturbing moral defect in Kenya today are unmistakable.

Since the olden days, the church is expected to be the patron of morality in society. It has played this role perfectly; until recently.

Today, the leadership of the church in Kenya seems to have forgotten this basic yet essential role. The clergy is a faded shadow of the pioneer leadership that made the church a greatly respected entity.

They are a far cry from the days when everyone, including the president and other top-ranking government officials, stood still and listened keenly whenever the likes of Archbishop Ndingi Mwana a’Nzeki, Archbishop David Gitari, and Reverend Timothy Njoya opened their lips to speak.

These were fearless men who stayed true to their calling and the role of the Christian church to uphold high moral values in society.

They preached the true meaning of the gospel and defended the citizens from any form of human rights violations by speaking openly and daringly to power.

But there is nothing to write home about the current crop of church leadership. The church is not only guilty of silence and turning a blind eye to the ills facing our great nation; it is directly involved in perpetuating some of the vices.

We are talking about church leaders who cannot take a stand and defend it amid public pressure. We are talking about leaders who cannot speak without appearing to be masters of doublespeak.

Mid last year, Anglican Bishop Joseph Ole Sapit, flanked by several members of the multi-sectoral initiative against corruption, made a public statement declaring that donations by politicians would no longer be allowed in churches.

“I urge all the bishops not to allow partisan politics in the pulpit. Let us not allow harambee monies to be a subtle way of sanitizing corrupt leaders,” he stated, further stating that fundraisers had become a fashionable avenue for politicians to hijack pulpits and misuse them to speak divisive politics.

The contentious declaration was openly supported by, among others, the Catholic church.

But Sapit appeared to have a change of heart shortly after, going to great lengths to explain that the church was not opposed to receiving donations from politicians, but the manner in which this was done.

He would around the same time preside over a fundraising event where politicians helped raise millions of shillings.

Church harambees have been done since time immemorial, and are a good gesture.

The problem is when politicians and other ‘outsiders’ use ill-gotten funds to brazenly buy pulpit time and votes in churches.

Senior politicians, including former prime minister Raila Odinga and former vice president Kalonzo Musyoka have publicly warned that churches have become avenues for laundering money.

While such claims are subject to confirmation by the relevant legal authorities, that such statements are being made about the holy institution shows that the church is no longer the respected body that it should be.

The church should be seen to stand for what is right and support calls for transparency on wealth sources.

Corruption is rampant in Kenya and our politicians are the biggest players. We have had senior politicians named in various questionable dealings that have led to loss of billions in taxpayers’ money.

These politicians flock churches at any given opportunity carrying bags of money.

The leadership in the church has the moral authority to question these leaders and demand that they declare the sources of the donations, particularly given that many politicians donate more than what they officially earn.

If that is done, the church will be sending the message that God only accepts giving from clean-acquired wealth.

The younger generation and the congregation will understand that giving should be clean, and not that giving cleanses. Instead of blindly receiving large donations of money, church leaders should speak boldly against corruption and aid investigative agencies where necessary.

The original teaching of Christianity was that good moral values such as honesty, hard work and humility are rewarded by God.

Most religious leaders today run what can be termed as psychological extortion centres in the name of worship centres.

You will watch television programmes where pastors do not preach anything outside the message of giving. Believers have been made to believe that by giving only, one will stumble upon riches as a reward.

The correct message should be that after hard work, remember to tithe. It’s a digital era, and going online, you will not miss people lamenting how congregants give and languish in poverty, while the church leaders are wallowing in wealth.

There have been undisputable exposes’ showing how some churches stage “miracles” with the aim of gaining a following and collecting money.

One was reported to have staged the healing of chronic diseases and physical conditions by paying and coaching prostitutes to deliver powerful yet false testimonies.

This is psychological extortion because these episodes are accompanied by calls to pay a certain amount of money to receive a certain ‘miracle’.

When millions of jobless poor Kenyans are assured of a financial breakthrough in a few days, they will hopefully part with their last coin and sit pretty waiting for the “miracle”.

The church should teach the value of hard work and faithfulness to God.

The most effective way to change society is to correctly guide the heart of human beings.

The church and other religious bodies should take that as their main role. By restoring order, dignity, and respect to the house of God, they will guide society in the right direction.

Firmness against compromise should be a quality among all the religious leaders in the country. That way, young people and society, in general, will take the church and its teaching seriously and the holy house can effectively play its role of guiding in good practices.
