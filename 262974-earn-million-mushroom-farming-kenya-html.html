Mushroom farming in Kenya: Requirements, capital, market

At first, it may seem that mushroom farming in Kenya is a difficult and tiresome agribusiness. However, if you dive into the specificity of mushroom growing, you will be able to see that there are many benefits. As it is quite a rare type of agribusiness in Kenya, you can profit from it in the shortest terms, and there will always be demand from mushroom buyers in Kenya.

Mushrooms were traditionally picked from the natural environment, and farmers did not grow them for commercial purposes. Today, mushroom farming has become a popular source of income for many youths worldwide. The crop is increasingly becoming an alternative protein to the famous red meat.

Mushroom farming in Kenya

Mushroom farming is one of the most successful agribusinesses not only in Kenya but also across the world. There are about 1.5 million mushroom species globally, with only 30 species domesticated as edible and medicinal mushrooms. Depending on the species, Mushrooms take half to two months to mature. However, some species, like shiitake, can take up to 3 months.

According to the National Farmers Information Service survey, Kenya produces approximately 500 tonnes of mushrooms annually. However, the demand for them is 1200 tonnes. This implies Kenya has to import about 700 tonnes of mushrooms from other countries to sustain the market demand.

Mushrooms have a high nutritional value. They are rich in nutrients and vitamins such as Vitamins B1, B2 and C. They also contain sodium, calcium, iron, copper, potassium and phosphorus minerals. Additionally, they are a good source of proteins and contain low cholesterol levels. Mushroom delicacy helps in boosting the body's immune system.

According to some experts, mushrooms can ease high blood pressure and regulation of sugar levels. This could explain why there is high demand for the crop. For you to be a successful mushroom farmer in Kenya, it is imperative to undertake mushroom farming training in Kenya.

Types of mushrooms

There are different types of mushrooms grown in Kenya. Some are edible, while others are not. Below are some of the edible mushrooms grown in Kenya.

  • Termitomyces
  • Shiitake
  • Portobello (brown)
  • Straw mushroom
  • Oyster
  • Button (White)
  • Chinese

Button and Oyster are the most common type of mushrooms grown in the country. The button takes about two months to mature. However, they are prone to diseases and infections, unlike oyster mushrooms which are disease resistant.

Requirements for mushroom farming

To start mushroom growing in Kenya, you must have a proper shelter, a constant supply of clean water and a warm temperature from 15 degrees to 30. To grow more than 2 tonnes, you will need 1/8 acre of land.

The harvest period repeats every two months if you are using a shelf-frame method. You also need small plastic bags to prepare the seedlings and large bags for the final planting.

Where to get mushroom seeds in Kenya

To get non-contaminated mushroom seeds, also known as mushroom spawns, you need to buy them from professional propagators or agencies like KARI or well-known institutions like JKUAT.

What are the challenges of mushroom farming in Kenya?

Before you venture into mushroom farming, it is important to know some of the challenges associated with its farming. Below are some of the challenges affecting growers.

  • One of the main challenges is that if you fail to pasteurise the mushroom substrate properly, the fungi develop moulds resulting in a loss. To avoid this, you can buy ready bags of freshly spawned substrate.
  • Mushroom farming is an expensive venture due to high input costs. They are also grown in specific parameters, unlike any other plant. It is also labour and energy-intensive.
  • Mushrooms are susceptible to mould, yeast, snails and rodent attacks, which affects the yields.
  • Lack of enough quality mushroom seeds (spawns).
  • Lack of proper skills in production and postharvest handling
  • A lean government extension service.
